# Executive Summary Gold Rules

Gold-standard rules for writing high-quality executive summaries, synthesized from authoritative writing-center and policy-brief sources. These rules are the shared writing contract for the document-distance test fixtures: two summaries written from the same source under these same rules should be semantically close, so the distance metric can be validated against a known-near pair. The rules deliberately favor clean, declarative, self-contained sentences that segment well into atomic statements.

## Purpose and scope

1. **Write it last** - draft the summary after the full document exists, so it distills the actual content, not anticipated conclusions
2. **Make it standalone** - the reader must grasp the full problem, findings, and recommendation without the source; no "see section 4.2" or "as detailed below"
3. **Serve decision-makers** - the audience is busy executives who may read this and nothing else; optimize every word for their decision

## Structure (required components)

4. **Lead with the problem** - open with a specific statement of the issue and why it matters; skip lengthy background
5. **Present findings as quantified facts** - state what was found with concrete numbers and results, not hedged impressions
6. **Order findings by descending significance** - most important first; each finding one to two sentences, leading with the insight then its single most relevant data point
7. **Close with one clear recommendation** - end with a specific, actionable next step the reader can act on; a missing recommendation is the most-cited failure mode
8. **Give the rationale** - connect the recommendation explicitly to the findings that justify it

All three of problem, findings, and recommendation must be present, whether as flowing narrative or labeled sections.

## Length and compression

9. **One page maximum** - roughly 200-500 words, or one to two dense paragraphs; never exceed two pages
10. **5-10% of the source length** - compress ruthlessly to stay within that envelope (some guides go as low as 2-5%)
11. **Cut redundancy and fluff** - remove filler ("very important", "among the major themes") and any sentence that restates the prior one; every sentence must advance the argument

## Content

12. **No new information** - stay within the source; do not speculate, extrapolate, or add interpretation beyond the evidence
13. **Objective tone** - third person, no editorializing ("I believe", "in my opinion")
14. **Plain language over jargon** - explain unavoidable terms briefly; assume an intelligent non-expert reader
15. **One main idea per paragraph** - a single focused claim per paragraph, no compound claims

## Style and voice

16. **Active voice, present tense** - "the data shows X", not "it is shown that X" or "the study revealed"
17. **Declarative, concise sentences** - favor subject-verb-object; cut every unnecessary word
18. **Lead each paragraph with its core claim** - state the point first, then support it

## Avoid

19. **No methodology, limitations, or caveats** - those belong in the full report; present findings as sound
20. **No hedging** - avoid "may", "could", "seems to", "arguably"; verify a claim against the source rather than weakening it
21. **No restating the source abstract or intro** - add value by synthesis, not repackaging

## Anti-patterns and common pitfalls

The failure modes most cited across sources, each with the fix.

- **Burying the lead** - the key finding sits mid-paragraph instead of first → lead with the single most important result
- **Information overload** - cramming every detail until the message drowns → keep only what drives the decision
- **No recommendation** - ending abruptly without an ask or next step (the most common blunder) → always close with a specific action
- **Jargon wall** - domain terminology that alienates the non-expert → plain language, explain any unavoidable term
- **Hedging** - "may", "could", "appears to" draining findings of force → state facts grounded in the evidence
- **Vague generalities** - "significant growth" with no number → quantify every claim
- **Restating the intro** - repackaging the source's abstract → synthesize, do not echo
- **Writing it first** - drafting before the document is done → write it last, from the finished work
- **Passive-voice fog** - "it was found that" hiding the actor → active voice, name who does what
- **Copy-paste from the body** - lifting paragraphs verbatim → compress into fresh, denser sentences
- **Too long** - exceeding one to two pages or the 5-10% envelope → cut to the budget
- **New or unsupported claims** - data or conclusions not in the source → never introduce information the source does not contain

## Consensus across sources

- Executive summaries are strategic documents that amplify the core message and drive decisions, not mechanical recaps
- Standalone readability and conciseness are non-negotiable - typically one page or one to two dense paragraphs (200-500 words)
- The three essential components are problem, findings/evidence, and recommended action
- Clear direct language beats jargon - a senior executive with no domain expertise should understand each claim in one read
- Methodology, caveats, and hedging are omitted; nuance belongs in the full report
