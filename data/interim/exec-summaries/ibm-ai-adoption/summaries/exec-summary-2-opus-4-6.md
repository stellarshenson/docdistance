# Executive Summary - Enterprise AI Adoption (v2, Opus 4.6)

Enterprise AI adoption has stalled at 42% active deployment among large organizations (over 1,000 employees), while 40% remain stuck in exploration or experimentation phases, unable to convert pilots into production—a gap that threatens competitive positioning as early adopters accelerate ahead. The primary finding is a widening divide: 59% of companies already deploying or exploring AI have accelerated their investments over the past 24 months, meaning early movers compound their advantage while laggards fall further behind. Limited AI skills and expertise is the single largest barrier, cited by 33% of respondents, followed by data complexity (25%) and ethical concerns (23%). Geographic disparities are stark—India (59%), the UAE (58%), and Singapore (53%) lead in active AI use, while France (26%), Australia (29%), and Spain (28%) lag significantly. Financial services leads industry adoption with roughly 50% deployment, while telecommunications trails at 37%. Generative AI introduces additional friction: 57% of non-adopters cite data privacy and 43% cite trust and transparency as inhibitors. Only 34% of organizations currently reskill employees to work alongside AI, and 20% report lacking employees with the right skills entirely. Despite 85% of IT professionals agreeing that consumers prefer companies with transparent AI practices, fewer than half take concrete steps—only 27% actively reduce bias and only 37% track data provenance. The top drivers pulling organizations forward are more accessible AI tools (45%), cost-reduction imperatives (42%), and AI embedded in off-the-shelf software (37%). IT automation (33%), security and threat detection (26%), and AI governance (25%) are the most common deployed use cases.

Organizations should prioritize closing the skills gap through aggressive reskilling programs and simplified tooling, since limited expertise is the dominant barrier at 33%, yet only 34% of companies invest in workforce training—resolving this single constraint unlocks the largest cohort of stalled adopters before the early-mover advantage becomes insurmountable.
