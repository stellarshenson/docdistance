# Executive Summary - Enterprise AI Adoption (v1, Opus 4.6)

Enterprise AI adoption has stalled at 42% active deployment among large organizations (over 1,000 employees), while 40% remain stuck in exploration or experimentation phases, unable to move models into production. The gap between early adopters accelerating investment and organizations unable to cross deployment thresholds represents a widening competitive divide that demands immediate attention from business leaders.

Early adopters are pulling further ahead: 59% of companies already deploying or exploring AI have accelerated their investments in the past 24 months, with China (85%), India (74%), and the UAE (72%) leading acceleration rates while Canada (35%), Australia (38%), and the UK (40%) lag. Geographic disparities in active deployment are stark—India (59%) and the UAE (58%) far outpace France (26%), Australia (29%), and Spain (28%). Financial services leads industry adoption at roughly 50% active deployment versus 37% in telecommunications. The primary barriers blocking the next wave are a skills gap (33% cite limited AI expertise), data complexity (25%), and ethical concerns (23%). One in five organizations lacks employees with the right skills, and 16% cannot find qualified new hires. Despite 85% of IT professionals agreeing that consumers prefer companies with transparent AI practices, fewer than half have taken concrete steps: only 27% actively reduce bias, 37% track data provenance, and 44% have developed ethical AI policies. Generative AI faces even steeper trust barriers, with 57% of non-adopters citing data privacy and 43% citing transparency concerns. Cost reduction and process automation (42%) and more accessible tools (45%) are the top adoption drivers, while R&D (44%) and workforce reskilling (39%) are the leading investment areas—yet only 34% of organizations currently train employees to work alongside AI.

Organizations should prioritize closing the skills gap through structured reskilling programs and simplified deployment tooling, since workforce readiness is the single largest barrier and only one-third of companies actively address it despite acceleration in accessible AI tools that make deployment increasingly feasible for prepared teams.
